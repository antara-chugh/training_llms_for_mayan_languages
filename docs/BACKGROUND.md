## Introduction

Indigenous migrant communities in the United States face significant barriers to language access, preventing civic engagement in these communities. Translation services for Indigenous migrant languages are hindered due to limited human translators, government funding, and long waiting periods for translations. This work evaluates the potential of post-training methods to increase performance on English-to-Q'anjob'al translation, one of many Mayan languages, building on prior research in transfer learning and curriculum learning for low-resource languages.

## Summary of "Impact Evaluation of AI-Language Access Models for Indigenous Language Communities in the City of Los Angeles"

This project builds directly on findings from a co-authored paper presented at the 2025 IEEE International Symposium on Ethics in Engineering, Science, and Technology (ETHICS) (Zeng et al., 2025). That paper evaluated AI-driven translation tools for Los Angeles City Council District 3's Indigenous language communities — speakers of Q'anjob'al, Ayuujk, Zapotec, Chinantec, and K'iche' — and proposed a framework centered on community engagement, technology and human capital investment, partnerships with technology providers and Indigenous organizations, and iterative evaluation.

Key findings relevant to this project:
- Los Angeles is home to over 34,000 Indigenous residents from Mexico and Central America, many of whom speak neither Spanish nor English.
- The city has identified 16 Emergency-Response Languages with limited translation and interpretation services, including Q'anjob'al.
- Existing language surveys often underrepresent Indigenous languages by misclassifying or grouping them into broader categories (e.g., "Latinx").
- Current language-access efforts are typically unpaid and community-based — including CIELO/USC's data-gathering work, UIUC's Q'anjob'al language materials project, and the Guatemalan-Maya Center's "Mayan Girls" translation program in Florida.
- AI-driven translation is promising but suffers from inaccuracies due to a lack of quality datasets; commercial tools like DeepL offer limited support for Indigenous languages as a result.
- Ethical implementation requires data sovereignty protocols, fair compensation for Indigenous contributors, and awareness that AI infrastructure's energy and environmental costs can disproportionately affect the same communities the tools aim to serve.
- Recommendations included establishing Indigenous Language Advisory Councils (ILACs) with real decision-making authority, balanced quantitative/qualitative evaluation (BLEU, BLEURT, BERTScore alongside community-defined cultural-preservation metrics), and phased implementation beginning with high-priority service areas.

This project picks up on the paper's technical constraints, addressing the limited amount of training data and post-training strategies for one Indigenous migrant language in particular, Q'anjob'al.


## Q'anjob'al Language & Language Access Challenges

Q'anjob'al is a Mayan language originating in Guatemala, and is the parent language of many other Mayan languages, including Chuj, Akateko, and Popti', often referred to as Q'anjob'lan languages (Maya Q'anjob'al Interpreting Services, 2025). Due to historical persecution and migration, many Q'anjob'al speakers now reside in Mexico and the continental United States. In the US, the largest Q'anjob'al communities can be found in Los Angeles, California and Champaign County, Illinois, where communities struggle with civic engagement due to language access barriers (Zeng et al., 2025): though the City of Los Angeles issued a mandate to increase access to government materials in Q'anjob'al back in 2021, significant language barriers still exist, and the city is not able to translate government documents into Q'anjob'al at scale (Zeng et al., 2025). Nationally, Q'anjob'al is one of the most common languages spoken in immigration court, yet there remains a significant lack of human translators for these plaintiffs (Medina, 2019). These gaps have led to the spread of misinformation in Indigenous communities during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, wrongful deportations, and the denial of asylum (Medina, 2019).

## Community-Driven Language Access Initiatives

Increasing language access for Indigenous migrant languages has primarily been driven by community and institutional efforts. In the city of Los Angeles, CIELO partnered with the USC Equity Research Institute to increase the amount of public data available for Indigenous migrant communities in the city. Through community surveys, the group identified 36 distinct Indigenous migrant languages spoken in Los Angeles (CIELO & USC Equity Research Institute, n.d.). Prompted by CIELO's efforts, the City of Los Angeles passed Executive Order 32, 2021 — Strengthening Language Access in the City of Los Angeles: Mandated Data, mandating increased data collection and translation for Q'anjob'al and other Indigenous migrant languages. The University of Illinois has established the Illinois Maya Institute to facilitate ethical engagement with Mayan communities and align research efforts. Q'anjob'al is further offered as coursework to students, and collaborations with community organizations, including Pixan' Konob', have helped create teaching resources to teach English speakers about Q'anjob'al (Center for Latin American & Caribbean Studies, n.d.-a). Finally, the Guatemalan Ministry of Education and USAID have worked to provide translations of children's books written in Spanish or English to Q'anjob'al, publicly available on the Bloom Library website (Bloom Library, n.d.; Ministerio de Educación de Guatemala, n.d.).

## Computational Linguistic Techniques & NLP Progress in Indigenous Latin American Languages

Overall, NLP research for Indigenous Latin American languages is limited, but gradually increasing (Simons et al., 2022). Research is limited both globally and locally — in Guatemala, local NLP efforts cover only two Indigenous languages, and Q'anjob'al has not been represented in these efforts at all (Tonja et al., 2024).

Significant advances in NLP for Indigenous Latin American languages have been made through the AmericasNLP competition, now in its fourth iteration. The competition has focused on translating between Spanish and 13 Indigenous languages, creating educational materials for Indigenous languages, and developing new evaluation metrics for translation into three Indigenous languages — Guaraní, Bribri, and Nahuatl — and includes one Mayan language, Maya. Over the course of the competition, researchers have shown the generalizability of low-resource MT strategies, finding that the most effective strategy for translation into Indigenous languages is fine-tuning a highly multilingual pretrained model, while for translation tasks into Spanish, decoder-style GPT-based models outperform smaller models (AmericasNLP, 2025).

Further work by Lou et al. (2024) introduces MayanV, a publicly released collection of curated Mayan-Spanish parallel corpora spanning several Mayan languages of Guatemala and Southern Mexico, built from official, community-produced materials. The authors trained bilingual and multilingual translation models from scratch and fine-tuned Meta's NLLB-200 on the corpora, demonstrating that multilingual training offers some performance gains, and that most currently available Mayan resources do not align with real-world usage.

Many key issues remain in the development of NLP systems for Indigenous Latin American languages, including lack of access to resources, lack of effort from scientific communities, and lack of Indigenous language speaker involvement in creating datasets (AmericasNLP, 2025). Indigenous communities also raise concerns about their lack of inclusion in research efforts, and about limited interest from government entities in increasing resources for these communities (Tonja et al., 2024; CIELO & USC Equity Research Institute, n.d.; Zeng et al., 2025).

## Transfer Learning Rationale

Work in low-resource NLP has shown that large-scale multilingual pretraining can substantially improve performance when parallel data is scarce. Following Zoph et al. (2016), this project first trains on a higher-resource language pair before adapting to the target low-resource language, for two reasons: **stability**, since gradient updates on an unfamiliar, low-resource domain tend to be noisy on their own, and **positive parameter transfer**, since the model has already learned generalizable structure it can build on rather than starting from scratch. At sufficient scale, large multilingual pretraining also lets a model map similar concepts across different languages into similar regions of its embedding space — a property demonstrated by XLM-R, which significantly outperforms mBERT on major multilingual benchmarks and delivers particularly large gains for low-resource languages (Conneau et al., 2020).

Building on these insights, this project fine-tunes open-source multilingual models on Q'anjob'al and larger Mayan corpora to assess the extent to which intra-family transfer learning benefits Q'anjob'al specifically — using 13 Mayan languages combined as the higher-resource pair, rather than a single high-resource language as in prior work, on the hypothesis that shared grammatical structure and roots across the Mayan family will boost overall Q'anjob'al performance.
